## Gaps that university syllabi often leave implicit

Even in strong programmes, **package publication and registry mechanics** (semantic versioning policy in practice, deprecation playbooks, dependency resolution edge cases, and multi‑language packaging ecosystems) are often under‑specified in course descriptions; the closest you tend to see is “maintenance & evolution,” “breaking changes,” and “CI/CD tooling,” rather than formal treatment of artifact repositories and public API stability contracts. This is visible even in otherwise aligned courses like CC4401 (maintenance/evolution + CI tools) and TU Delft CSE1110 (breaking changes in open‑source) where “versioning policy as product contract” is usually implied rather than foregrounded. citeturn12view6turn20view1

Likewise, **plugin architectures and extension ecosystems** are frequently taught as architecture patterns (product lines, middleware frameworks, microservices gateways/registries) rather than as a “library plugin system” per se; UC’s IIC2173 and the Rzeszów microservices syllabus are valuable precisely because they name product lines/configuration management and system components (gateway, registry, messaging, containerisation) explicitly. citeturn12view2turn24view1

Finally, **contract testing** (consumer‑driven contracts like Pact) is rarely named explicitly in university catalog blurbs, even when the underlying needs—API evolution, compatibility, integration verification—are covered through maintenance/evolution, CI/CD, and testing levels. Where university syllabi do get close, it is usually phrased as “contracts/contract changes” (Rzeszów) or “design by contract” (TU Delft) rather than “consumer‑driven contract testing.” citeturn24view1turn20view1
